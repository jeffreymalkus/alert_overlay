Critical Finding

44.3% of all trades (431 of 973) use a fixed R:R fallback target — not a real structural level. This means nearly half the simulation is pricing exits at arbitrary predetermined distances, not at actual resistance/support levels.

The structural target functions (compute_structural_target_long/short) try to find real levels (VWAP, session high/low, PDH/PDL, measured moves). When no viable structural level exists within 1.0–3.0 R:R, they return a fixed_rr_fallback at the strategy's default R:R (1.5, 2.0, or 3.0).

3. What This Means

How the simulation currently works

Each strategy calls compute_structural_target_long() with real price levels as candidates (VWAP, session high/low, PDH/PDL, measured moves, etc.). The function finds the nearest viable level within 1.0–3.0 R:R.

When a structural level exists: target_price = that level, actual_rr = real distance / risk. Good — this is a real exit.

When NO structural level exists within range: target_price = entry + fallback_rr × risk, actual_rr = fallback_rr (e.g., 2.0). Bad — this is a fabricated exit point with no structural basis.

The double problem

Problem 1 — Fixed targets are unrealistic: 431 trades (44.3%) exit at arbitrary R:R multiples. There's no actual resistance/support at those prices. The simulation credits full target R:R (minus friction) when price touches these phantom levels.

Problem 2 — Stops are too tight for friction: Even for the 542 structurally-targeted trades, median stop distances of 0.46%–1.68% mean 16bps round-trip costs consume 10%–35% of the risk budget.

4. Impact on Simulation Accuracy

What happens in simulate_strategy_trade()

When target_rr comes from a fixed fallback (e.g., 2.0), and the simulation reaches b.high >= target, it awards pnl_rr = 2.0 - cost_rr. But there's no actual structural resistance at that price — the real market might blow right through it or reverse well before.

This means target hits for fixed-fallback trades are essentially random price touches being credited as successful exits. Win rates on these trades are inflated.

Quantifying the distortion

Of 973 total trades:

  • 542 structural (55.7%) — target is a real level. Exit behavior is defensible.
  • 431 fixed fallback (44.3%) — target is fictional. Wins at these levels are coincidental.

The simulation's PF and total R are a blend of honest trades and fantasy exits. The true system performance is likely worse than even the friction-adjusted numbers show.

5. Recommended Path Forward

Immediate actions

  1. Split results by target type: Re-run replay showing PF/WR/TotalR separately for structural vs fixed-fallback trades. This shows the "real" performance of structural exits vs the noise from fixed ones.
  2. Consider removing fixed fallback entirely: If no structural level exists within 1.0–3.0 R:R, skip the trade. A trade with no visible exit target probably shouldn't be taken.
  3. Widen stop economics: As previously identified, median risk % needs to be higher so friction is a smaller portion of R.
